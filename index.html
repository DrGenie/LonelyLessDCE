LonelyLessAustralia Decision Aid Tool

Structured scenarios for reducing loneliness among older adults in Australia, using discrete choice experiment evidence on what older adults value.

Loneliness intervention Policy decision support

Why this tool

Loneliness affects over five million Australians, particularly older adults (65 years and above), with important consequences for mental health, physical health and service use. This tool summarises discrete choice experiment results to predict programme uptake under alternative configurations and links these to simple cost and QALY based benefit calculations.

The design follows the same structured, government facing layout as the STEPS FETP tool, so that results can be used directly in policy and commissioning discussions.

How to use this tool

  1. Review the Attributes tab to understand each programme feature. Hover over the info icons for details and reference levels.
  2. Configure your scenario in the Inputs tab using the level cards. All features (except method) are required. If no method is selected, in person is assumed.
  3. Click Calculate & View Results to run the model with your chosen configuration.
  4. Use the WTP Results tab to see how much older adults are willing to pay for changes in each attribute.
  5. Use the Predicted Programme Uptake tab to see uptake versus non uptake and a narrative recommendation.
  6. Inspect the Costs & Benefits tab to view costs, QALYs and monetised benefits, including a combined bar chart.
  7. Use the Saved Scenarios tab to store, compare and export configurations as a short PDF briefing.

Willingness to pay (WTP) results

WTP for each attribute is calculated as the ratio of the attribute coefficient to the negative of the cost coefficient. This represents the additional out of pocket amount that older adults are willing to pay for a change in that attribute, holding other factors constant.

For example, community engagement has a WTP of A$14.47 (SE 3.31, p < 0.001), which means older adults are willing to pay around A$14.50 per session more for community based programmes relative to peer support.

Costs and benefits analysis

Modelling assumptions

The analysis uses a simple cohort of 250 older adults and applies QALY gains to those who are predicted to take up the programme. Monetary benefits are valued at A$50,000 per QALY, consistent with published cost effectiveness work on loneliness interventions.
Reference: Engel, L., Lee, Y. Y., Le, L. K. D., Lal, A., & Mihalopoulos, C. (2021). Reducing loneliness to prevent depression in older adults in Australia: a modelled cost effectiveness analysis. Mental Health and Prevention, 24. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mhp.2021.200212
