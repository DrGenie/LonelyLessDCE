What LonelyLessAustralia does

LonelyLessAustralia helps policy makers, service providers and partners explore options for scaling up support programmes that reduce loneliness among older adults in Australia. It links three main inputs:

  • Preferences and willingness-to-pay estimates from a discrete choice experiment with older adults and key stakeholders.
  • Costing templates for different programme types, delivery methods and levels of intensity.
  • Simple outcome multipliers that translate uptake into changes in loneliness, mental health and quality of life.

Users can set a programme configuration, see expected uptake, willingness to pay, costs and benefit–cost ratios, and compare saved scenarios side by side for business case and commissioning discussions.

The tool is designed to support transparent conversations with health services, aged care providers, local governments and community organisations. It keeps assumptions visible and connects preference evidence directly to programme design and economic and social summaries.

How to use the tool

  1. Go to the Configuration tab and choose: type of support programme, method of engagement, frequency of interaction, duration of each interaction, accessibility/convenience and the out-of-pocket cost per session.
  2. Keep the cost slider within the cost range used in the discrete choice experiment (Free, AUD 5, AUD 20 and AUD 50 per session) so that uptake predictions remain valid.
  3. The tool uses the mixed logit preference model estimated from the loneliness DCE. There is a single preference model, so no model selection is required.
  4. Click Apply configuration to update all calculations, then use View results summary to open a scenario summary with headline recommendations.
  5. Save promising scenarios in the Saved scenarios tab and download them as Excel or a policy brief PDF for reporting and funding submissions.
  6. For workshops or sensitivity checks, use the Population simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs to explore how results change with scale, uptake and different benefit definitions, and the Advanced and methods tab to adjust multipliers and open the full technical appendix.

LonelyLessAustralia is designed for laptops, desktops, tablets and mobiles.

Attributes and levels in the loneliness preference study

The discrete choice experiment asked older adults and stakeholders to choose between alternative loneliness support programmes and an opt-out option. Each programme was described by the attributes below. LonelyLessAustralia keeps programme configurations inside the experimental range so that results remain consistent with the underlying mixed logit model.

Opt-out and cost templates

Element Definition How LonelyLessAustralia uses it
Opt-out alternative
An opt-out option where the specific loneliness support programme under consideration is not funded.
The discrete choice experiment included an explicit opt-out option. LonelyLessAustralia uses this as the benchmark when calculating uptake. Uptake is the predicted share choosing the programme configuration rather than opting out.
Cost templates
Template budgets submitted by programme partners for different programme types and configurations.
Templates are used as alternative settings rather than being combined. Policy makers can switch between templates to see how cost structure affects economic cost while holding programme design constant.
Opportunity cost of staff and volunteer time
The value of staff and volunteer time spent on the programme instead of other activities, per participant per month.
When the opportunity cost toggle is on, LonelyLessAustralia adds this to programme costs to yield total economic cost. When off, outputs focus on budgetary costs only.
Preference model
Mixed logit model from the loneliness discrete choice experiment.
All uptake and willingness-to-pay estimates are based on a single mixed logit model that uses the attributes and levels described above. There are no latent classes in the loneliness DCE; sensitivity analysis focuses on cost, uptake and benefit assumptions instead.

Reference attribute levels form the baseline. Alternative-specific constants capture layout effects and the tendency to choose or reject programmes in general. LonelyLessAustralia uses the opt-out constant when predicting overall uptake of any loneliness support option versus no funded programme.

These percentages are obtained by plugging the chosen configuration into the mixed logit model and comparing the utility of the programme with the utility of opting out. They summarise how attractive the configuration is to respondents in the preference study.

Support loneliness programme -

Estimated share of respondents who would support funding this programme rather than keeping the status quo.

Choose opt-out -

Willingness to pay and economic outcomes

Willingness to pay (WTP) per participant per session is derived from the preference model by dividing attribute coefficients by the cost coefficient. It indicates how much value respondents attach to the bundle of attributes in this configuration relative to a baseline. Total WTP aggregates this value across all participants and sessions for each group and at population scale.

WTP per participant per month -

Indicative value respondents place on this configuration per participant per month, relative to the baseline design.

Total WTP per group -

WTP per participant per month multiplied by participants per group and programme duration, treating preference evidence as an additional benefit measure.

Programme cost per group -

Direct programme cost per group before adding any opportunity cost of staff or volunteer time.

Total economic cost per group -

Programme cost plus opportunity cost where applicable. This is the main cost figure used in benefit–cost ratios.

Net benefit per group (social outcomes) -

Indicative social benefit per group minus total economic cost per group, combining improvements in loneliness and related outcomes. WTP is reported separately as a preference-based benefit metric.

Benefit–cost ratio (per group) -

Total indicative social benefit per group divided by total economic cost per group. Values above one suggest that benefits exceed costs on the assumptions used.

Social outcome implications

Participants reached and social outcomes are obtained by combining uptake with multipliers on group size and number of groups. Indicative benefit per group converts improvements in loneliness and related outcomes into monetary terms using values that can be adjusted in the Advanced and methods tab.

Participants reached (all groups) -

Estimated number of participants who complete the programme across all planned groups, adjusting for uptake and completion rates.

Meaningful connections supported per year -

Approximate number of sustained social connection episodes or equivalent outcome units per year once programmes are running at scale.

Indicative social benefit per group -

Monetary value of improved loneliness, mental health and quality of life per group using the values specified in the Advanced and methods tab.

Cost templates and sources

Cost templates are based on submissions from programme partners and service providers for different types and configurations of loneliness support. Templates are used as alternative settings rather than being combined.

Policy makers can switch between templates to see how cost structure affects economic cost while holding programme design constant. Templates that include the opportunity cost of staff or volunteer time feed into total economic cost when the opportunity cost switch is on.

When the opportunity cost switch is off, results focus on direct budgetary costs only.

Sensitivity and DCE benefit summary

This tab brings together preference-based benefits from the discrete choice experiment and the cost and social outcome modules. It shows the total willingness-to-pay benefit associated with each configuration, the share that is linked to improvements in loneliness and related outcomes, and summary cost–benefit indicators such as the benefit–cost ratio and net present value.

Each row in the table represents a scenario, starting with the current configuration and followed by saved scenarios. For each scenario, LonelyLessAustralia recomputes uptake, economic cost per group, willingness-to-pay based benefits, social outcome benefits and combined benefit–cost metrics using the mixed logit preference model and the opportunity cost setting.

Full formulas and worked numerical examples are set out in the technical appendix, including how willingness to pay is constructed from the discrete choice experiment and how social outcome benefits are linked to improvements in loneliness. About LonelyLessAustralia

LonelyLessAustralia is part of a broader programme of work on loneliness and social isolation among older adults in Australia. The tool provides a structured way to test programme options and explain cost, benefit and willingness-to-pay implications in policy discussions.

The tool is designed to support health and aged-care departments, local governments, primary health networks and community organisations as they plan investments in loneliness support. It helps answer questions such as:

  • How do costs, willingness to pay and social benefits change when moving across different types of support programmes?
  • What happens to uptake when the method of engagement, frequency, duration or accessibility changes?
  • How many groups and participants are needed to reach population targets for reducing loneliness?

Prepared by Mesfin Genie, PhD, Newcastle Business School, The University of Newcastle, Australia, with partners. For questions or clarifications please contact mesfin.genie@newcastle.edu.au.

Advanced settings and assumptions

This panel allows technical users to adjust key multipliers for workshops without editing code. Changes affect calculations immediately in this browser session and are reflected in the Results, Population simulation and Sensitivity / DCE benefits tabs.
