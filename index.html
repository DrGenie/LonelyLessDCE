LonelyLessAustralia Decision Aid Tool

A policy decision aid to evaluate loneliness support programs for older adults in Australia, combining discrete choice evidence, realistic costs and health economic outcomes.

Pilot policy tool For governments and health planners

What this tool does

The tool brings together discrete choice experiment results, cost data and health outcomes to give a coherent picture of an intervention's financial viability and social value. It is designed for health departments, primary health networks, local governments and community organisations that are planning or scaling loneliness interventions for older adults.

It helps answer questions such as whether older adults are likely to join a particular program, which features matter most to them, and how much impact in health and economic terms can be expected relative to the costs. Users can configure program features, see projected uptake, estimate willingness to pay based benefits and quantify cost savings and return on investment.

Key questions for decision makers

  • Will older adults in my region actually enrol in this program configuration
  • Which design elements drive interest and which ones reduce participation
  • What are the likely health and quality of life gains, including loneliness free days
  • What is the value of these benefits in monetary terms from user preferences and from health economics
  • How do total economic costs compare with benefits and cost savings over time

Evidence base

The tool combines a discrete choice experiment on loneliness support programs with Australian cost effectiveness work on older adult loneliness interventions and recent evidence on the economic cost of loneliness and health service use. Detailed sources and assumptions are documented in the technical appendix.

This prototype is intended for policy exploration and scenario analysis. It does not replace full business case development or jurisdiction specific costing.

Overview

Loneliness among older adults in Australia has measurable health and economic consequences. Evidence suggests that lonely older adults have higher risks of depression, dementia, stroke and early mortality, and that loneliness is associated with greater use of health care and productivity losses. At population level, the economic cost of loneliness in Australia has been estimated in the billions of dollars per year.

Community based programs that support social connection can reduce loneliness and help prevent depression. Modelled evaluations for older Australians find that such interventions can produce positive returns on investment when health care cost savings and productivity gains are included. For example, one national scale educational friendship program for older women showed that for each dollar invested, savings of more than two dollars were projected over five years, together with millions of loneliness free days gained.

This decision aid uses discrete choice experiment estimates to predict programme uptake and willingness to pay, and links these to realistic cost components and health outcomes. It aims to support transparent discussion between policy analysts, clinicians and community partners about which program configurations are most attractive and where investments may yield the greatest value.

How to use the tool

  1. Start with the Attributes tab to understand each program feature and its reference level.
  2. Open the Configuration tab and give your scenario a clear name and notes.
  3. Select the program type, delivery method, frequency, duration, accessibility and participant cost.
  4. Choose whether to adjust costs for state specific cost of living and whether to include opportunity cost of participant time.
  5. Click Apply configuration to calculate uptake and economic outcomes, and review the headline recommendation and briefing text.
  6. Use the WTP outcomes, Programme uptake and Costs and benefits tabs to explore results in more detail.
  7. Save scenarios for later comparison and export a PDF summary for reports and stakeholder meetings.
  8. Consult the technical appendix for a full description of methods, assumptions and evidence sources.

Key attributes and levels

The program attributes and levels in this tool are based on a discrete choice experiment with older adults in Australia. For each attribute, one level acts as the reference and the others represent improvements or trade offs that drive willingness to pay and uptake.

Cost per session

Out-of-pocket fee that participants pay per session, in Australian dollars. This is treated as a continuous attribute from 0 to 100 dollars per session.

Accessibility

Reference level is support at home. Configurable levels are a local venue in the nearby area or a wider community location that may require longer travel.

Frequency of interaction

Reference level is daily contact. Configurable levels are weekly or monthly structured sessions. Higher frequency generally increases perceived value but may reduce feasibility.

Method of interaction

Reference level is in-person delivery. Alternatives include fully virtual sessions or hybrid models that combine in-person and online components.

Duration of each interaction

Reference level is a 30 minute interaction. Configurable levels are 2 hour sessions and 4 hour sessions, which affect both effectiveness and opportunity cost of time.

Type of support programme

Reference level is peer support. Configurable options include community engagement activities, structured psychological support and virtual reality based programs.

Willingness to pay outcomes

Willingness to pay values show how much older adults are prepared to pay for an improvement in each attribute relative to its reference level, based on trade offs in the discrete choice experiment. The values are expressed as dollars per participant per session.

For a given configuration, the tool aggregates the relevant willingness to pay contributions across attributes, multiplies by the number of sessions and participants, and reports a willingness to pay based benefit measure. This can be compared with programme costs to obtain a benefit cost ratio grounded in participant preferences.

Predicted programme uptake

The discrete choice experiment is used to estimate the probability that an older adult chooses the configured programme instead of the opt out option. The model converts this individual probability into a projected uptake percentage for a cohort of eligible participants.

QALY benefits

QALY gains are based on modelled reductions in loneliness and depression among older adults. The tool multiplies QALY gain per participant by the number of participants and a monetary value per QALY, which is configurable in the technical appendix.

Cost savings and productivity gains

Health system cost savings and productivity gains are based on Australian modelling of a national friendship enrichment program for older women, which reported healthcare and productivity savings over three and five year time horizons. The tool scales these per participant savings to the configured cohort size.

Loneliness free days

Loneliness free days are an interpretable outcome that captures the number of days during which participants are expected not to experience loneliness. The tool uses estimates from Australian modelling that reported millions of loneliness free days gained over five years, and applies a per participant figure to the configured cohort.

WTP based benefits

Willingness to pay based benefits are derived directly from the discrete choice experiment by summing attribute specific willingness to pay estimates for the configuration and multiplying by sessions and participants. This provides a preference based measure of programme value that is distinct from QALY and cost saving metrics.
